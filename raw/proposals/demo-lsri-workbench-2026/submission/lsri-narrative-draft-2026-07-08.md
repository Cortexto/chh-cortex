# AI-Supported Digital Health Navigation and Conversational Analytics for Refugee Health Service Utilization

**Lead PI:** Shatha Elnakib, Center for Humanitarian Health, Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health

**Hypothesis (open — reconcile with Aral + Shatha before final PDF):** Hybrid Fork B + Fork C: test whether AI-mediated digital health tools increase utilization of general and public health services among refugee and returnee populations, using conversational health data as a novel implementation-science evidence modality versus survey-only workflows.

---

## 1. Transformative Potential and Impact

Humanitarian health programs still rely heavily on structured survey instruments to understand needs, barriers, and health-seeking behavior in displacement settings. These tools capture predefined fields but miss conversational nuance: how people describe symptoms, express mistrust, navigate entitlement complexity, or decide when to seek facility-based care. At the same time, AI-assisted digital health tools are spreading in stable health systems, yet rigorous evidence on their use in humanitarian response remains thin, especially for mobile refugee and returnee populations who cannot be served by fixed-facility models alone.

This project proposes a bounded, high-risk catalytic study at the intersection of public health, implementation science, and data science: whether AI-supported digital health navigation, paired with systematic analysis of de-identified conversational health data, can increase utilization of general and public health services among refugee and returnee populations in resource-limited settings. The scientific contribution is twofold. First, it treats operational conversational datasets as a new evidence modality for humanitarian maternal and reproductive health, complementing rather than replacing survey-based workflows. Second, it applies rapid-cycle implementation learning appropriate to humanitarian timelines, rather than defaulting to slow, large-scale trials ill-suited to a two-year Individual Award.

The Center for Humanitarian Health (CHH) at Johns Hopkins is positioned to lead this evidence generation. HERA Digital Health, a humanitarian digital health organization with operational reach among displaced populations, provides a bounded data partnership (in-kind access and technical support within the ≤10% subaward cap). This award does not fund large-scale field implementation; it seeds the science and evaluation architecture that could support follow-on external funding.

**Translational impact:** Better targeting of digital navigation interventions; actionable intelligence on barriers to public-health service use; trainee pipeline through a DrPH dissertation thread; publication pathway on AI in humanitarian health evidence.

## 3. Strength of Approach and Feasibility

**Scientific question:** Among refugee and returnee populations in settings with limited resources or strained health infrastructure, does exposure to AI-supported digital health navigation improve utilization of general and public health services relative to pre-intervention or comparison benchmarks, and what implementation factors explain uptake?

**Design:** Mixed-methods implementation science over 24 months. We do not propose a large RCT; the program does not require one. Instead:

| Phase | Activity | Output |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Year 1, Q1–Q2 | IRB protocol; de-identification pipeline; baseline utilization metrics | Approved protocol; analytic dataset |
| Year 1, Q3–Q4 | Deploy or observe bounded digital navigation module; rapid-cycle check-ins | Cycle 1 learning memo |
| Year 2 | NLP/thematic analysis of conversational data; linkage to utilization indicators | Peer-reviewable methods + findings |

**Data sources:** De-identified conversational health data from HERA humanitarian operations (latent asset; reduces field startup). Where feasible, comparison to survey-based benchmarks (e.g., structured data collection workflows). **Geography:** Initial analytic focus on refugee/returnee corridors where CHH and partners have access; specific site selection will follow IRB and partner agreement review before naming in the final submission.

**AI / data science methods:**
- NLP and thematic coding of health-seeking narratives
- Pattern detection for barriers (entitlement confusion, facility access, trust)
- Bias and safety review of AI-mediated outputs
- JHU-hosted compute (allowable per RFP)

**Ethics:** All conversational data handled under JHU IRB with explicit de-identification, no PII/PHI in shared analytic artifacts, and protection-aware protocols for vulnerable populations.

**Feasibility:** JHU hosts analysis, faculty effort, trainee support, and compute. HERA provides data access and technical partnership within ≤10% subaward or in-kind. Prior quasi-experimental work on antenatal visit attendance in Istanbul migrant health centers (JMIR preprint under peer review; NCT05094518) demonstrates CHH-adjacent evaluation capacity, though that study used smartphone reminders rather than conversational AI and should not be over-claimed here.
