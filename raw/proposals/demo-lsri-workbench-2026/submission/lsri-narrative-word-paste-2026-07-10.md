# AI-Supported Digital Health Navigation and Conversational Analytics for Refugee Health Service Utilization

**Lead PI:** Shatha Elnakib, Center for Humanitarian Health (CHH), Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health

## Central Hypothesis and Specific Aims

**Central hypothesis.** Routinely generated, de-identified conversational data from an operating digital health service contain valid and actionable signals about modifiable barriers to maternal and public health service utilization among displaced populations represented in the approved study corpus. A navigation intervention designed around one validated barrier will be feasible, acceptable, and associated with improvement in prespecified navigation and utilization indicators.

**Aim 1. Establish and validate a governed conversational and utilization evidence pipeline.** We will audit the operational corpus, establish governance and de-identification under Johns Hopkins University Institutional Review Board (IRB) oversight, develop a barrier taxonomy through natural language processing and thematic coding, and validate conversational measures against structured and utilization data where available. Where the corpus permits, we will evaluate lay-language health terminology in underrepresented refugee-community languages, including dialectal Arabic. Outputs: governed dataset, validation results, terminology brief, and methods manuscript.

**Aim 2. Identify and prioritize modifiable barriers to health service utilization.** We will estimate barrier prevalence and associations with utilization patterns, compare conversational and structured evidence, and select one barrier using prespecified criteria: prevalence, modifiability, and equity relevance. Outputs: barrier evidence profile, learning memo, and CHH-approved design target.

**Aim 3. Design and feasibility-test one AI-supported navigation intervention targeting the priority barrier.** We will translate Aim 2 findings into requirements, develop one bounded HERA-compatible prototype, and evaluate feasibility, acceptability, usability, fidelity, and change in prespecified utilization indicators. Subject to confirmation, the Johns Hopkins Center for Bioengineering Innovation and Design (CBID) would provide design-methodology oversight and a supervised student pathway. Outputs: prototype, design documentation, implementation findings, and estimates for follow-on study planning.

## 1. Transformative Potential and Impact

### Significance

The strongest existing evidence base concerns Syrian refugee women in Türkiye. Formal access includes Ministry of Health migrant health centers,[15,16] but language, navigation, fragmented records, cost, mistrust, and mobility can interrupt continuity of care.[1-3] Findings vary: a 2018 hospital cohort reported lower antenatal follow-up and several adverse outcomes, while a 2022 prospective high-risk-pregnancy study found no significant adjusted differences in live birth, preterm birth, or low birth weight.[4,5] A 2025 meta-analysis nevertheless identified persistent maternal and newborn health inequalities.[6] Together, the evidence supports a focus on modifiable access pathways rather than refugee status as a causal explanation.

Prior HERA work provides a scientific starting point. Its earlier application provided reminders, health information, record storage, emergency contact, and health-system navigation in Arabic, Turkish, and English.[1,2] A quasi-experimental evaluation under peer review found higher mean antenatal attendance among women with confirmed reminder exposure than in a retrospective clinic cohort, 2.5 versus 1.9 visits, but implementation problems prevented causal interpretation.[7] A Lebanon study also reported improved antenatal utilization, but its provider-training, messaging, and gamification components cannot be separated.[8] These studies support plausibility, not proof that conversational AI improves utilization.

A 2026 Nature Health study classified health-related intent in 617,827 de-identified consumer chatbot conversations but did not study a humanitarian population or link conversations to later care-seeking.[9] LSRI extends this method into governed humanitarian health research and tests whether conversational signals add useful evidence beyond structured and utilization measures; it does not assume superiority.

A 2024 review found that only six of 69 humanitarian sexual and reproductive health intervention studies explicitly used an implementation research framework.[14] This proposal connects corpus validation, barrier prioritization, intervention design, and implementation evaluation within 24 months.

### Innovation and Potential Impact

The project integrates privacy-preserving conversational analysis, validation against structured and utilization data, and translation of one validated barrier into a user-tested intervention. Conversational data may add no information, may reflect platform-selection or documentation bias, or may be insufficient for utilization linkage. A rigorous null finding would still define the method's limits.

The project treats language as a measurement and safety problem, not a generic translation task. WHO guidance and research on sex, gender, language, and cultural bias support governance, subgroup assessment, and local validation.[10-12] Health-chatbot evaluations also give limited attention to ethics and patient safety.[13] We will test whether lay-language terms align with clinician-reviewed navigation constructs and whether terminology failures create safety or comprehension risks.

If successful, the project will produce a tested path from governed conversations to a navigation response, peer-reviewable methods, a DrPH dissertation thread, a design handoff, and evidence for a larger study.

## 2. Strategic Alignment

The proposal addresses LSRI criteria for high-risk science, AI and data science, translational impact, student benefit, and follow-on funding. AI drives the analytic pipeline and navigation intervention; life-science relevance runs through maternal and public health utilization. DrPH research and the proposed CBID student pathway provide trainee benefit, while CHH retains scientific leadership.

## 3. Strength of Approach and Feasibility

### Aim 1 Approach: Governed Evidence Pipeline

**Design.** Aim 1 is a mixed-methods corpus characterization and validation study conducted during Months 1-12.

**Data and governance.** Candidate sources include de-identified HERA conversations and structured and utilization records where linkage is permitted. The audit will document population, languages, dates, unit of analysis, usable volume, completeness, de-identification, permissions, linkage, and fitness. Geography and transfer beyond the represented population will follow audit and IRB review.

**Measures and analysis.** Candidate utilization measures include antenatal visits, migrant health center registration and visits, referral completion, and sustained engagement. The primary hierarchy will be finalized before Aim 2 based on linkage. Conversational measures include barrier codes, thematic clusters, and terminology fidelity. A clinician-reviewed annotation subset will support inter-rater reliability and classification precision, recall, and F1. Convergence analyses will compare conversational and structured signals. Language evaluation will assess fidelity, comprehension, and safety where the corpus permits.

**Pitfalls and alternatives.** Incomplete linkage will narrow the analytic sample and precision. A null incremental-value result remains an Aim 1 finding. If language samples are insufficient, the project will report the evaluation protocol and descriptive results without claiming language-specific performance.

### Aim 2 Approach: Barrier Identification and Prioritization

**Design and analysis.** Aim 2 will use cross-sectional models and longitudinal analyses only where repeated linked observations exist. Models will match each outcome distribution and adjust for prespecified covariates. Conversational and structured evidence will be triangulated before selecting one target by prevalence, modifiability, and equity relevance.

**Pitfalls and alternatives.** Divergent signals will remain in the evidence profile and be assessed for measurement, platform-selection, or subgroup effects. Aim 3 will proceed only with an adequately supported, actionable barrier.

### Aim 3 Approach: Navigation Intervention and Feasibility Evaluation

**Design.** During Months 13-24, the team will translate the selected barrier into requirements, map the navigation journey, generate concepts, build one bounded HERA-compatible prototype, and conduct supervised testing. Possible forms include service matching, referral navigation, or proactive guidance.

**Implementation outcomes.** Feasibility, acceptability, appropriateness, usability, reach, and fidelity will use established instruments where applicable, including the Feasibility of Intervention Measure, Acceptability of Intervention Measure, Intervention Appropriateness Measure, and System Usability Scale. Utilization change will use pre-post comparison; interrupted time series will be used only with sufficient repeated measures. Estimates will inform follow-on design, not definitive causal claims.

**CBID work package.** Subject to confirmation, CBID would lead requirements synthesis, journey mapping, concept generation, prototyping, supervised usability work, and documentation. CHH retains research design, IRB, natural language processing, statistics, interpretation, and publication. HERA provides data interface, platform access, and bounded integration.

**Pitfalls and alternatives.** If CBID timing does not align, CHH and HERA will complete requirements and a low-fidelity prototype, with CBID advisory review if available. Insufficient utilization linkage will shift emphasis to implementation outcomes and parameters for a later evaluation.

### Timeline

- **Year 1, Quarters 1-2:** IRB preparation and review; de-identification and corpus audit; baseline utilization measures. Outputs: approved protocol and governed analytic dataset.
- **Year 1, Quarters 3-4:** Aim 1 validation; Aim 2 barrier analysis and prioritization; design brief. Outputs: coding framework with validation findings, terminology brief where supported, and selected design target.
- **Year 2, Quarters 1-2:** Aim 3 requirements, journey mapping, concept generation, and prototype development. Outputs: bounded prototype and design documentation.
- **Year 2, Quarters 3-4:** Supervised usability and feasibility evaluation; utilization linkage where available; dissemination. Outputs: implementation findings, effect estimates, methods manuscript, and follow-on study parameters.

### Feasibility

JHU will host oversight, analysis, trainee support, secure storage, and compute. HERA will provide candidate data, de-identification and platform support, and bounded integration within the 10% cap. Prior work demonstrates refugee-focused digital health research capacity and provides lessons about identifier capture and linkage.[1,2,7]

## 5. Future Funding Potential

The award is intended to generate the governed dataset, validated measures, implementation findings, and effect-size estimates required for a larger external study. Follow-on pathways include NIH or foundation implementation research, a Development Innovation Ventures application where stage and fit are appropriate, and CHH-led publications and external proposals on responsible AI and digital health in humanitarian settings.

## 6. Appropriate Use of Institutional Funds

Funds will prioritize JHU scientific and analyst effort, DrPH support, secure compute, prototyping, feasibility evaluation, and dissemination. CBID costs will enter the budget only after its mechanism and effort are confirmed. HERA funding will cover de-identification, data and platform access, and bounded integration within the 10% cap. JHU research administration will determine salary, fringe, indirect-cost, facility, and student-support assumptions.

## 7. Demonstrated Need for Support

LSRI support will convert an operational data asset into a governed research resource, test incremental value beyond structured measures, and generate feasibility and utilization estimates for a larger study. It allows CHH to establish methods, ethics, trainee development, and preliminary evidence while keeping external activity bounded.
