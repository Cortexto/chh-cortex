# From Service Data to Scientific Instrument: Measuring Self-Reported Health Service Utilization and Navigation Barriers among Refugee Women

**Lead PI:** Shatha Elnakib, Center for Humanitarian Health (CHH), Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health

## Central Hypothesis and Specific Aims

Routine health information systems and facility-derived indicators primarily reflect service contacts, not complete care-seeking pathways.[15] The interval from perceived need through referral to arrival is rarely measured prospectively in routine or survey systems.[3,10] Among participating refugee women, HERA, an operating AI-supported WhatsApp navigation service described under Significance, records questions, referrals, reminders, and structured self-reported attendance confirmations generated during this interval. HERA itself has been studied for feasibility, acceptability, and antenatal-attendance outcomes.[1,2,5] What remains untested is whether its routine records can function as research measures: structured follow-up responses as candidate self-reported utilization measures, and free-text conversations as evidence of perceived barriers. Referral messages document exposure, not action; silence is missing data. This award evaluates HERA records against what traditional instruments capture; a later powered study will compare the intervention with standard outreach or no tool.

**Central hypothesis.** Prospectively designed follow-up questions can produce reliable self-reported utilization measures among participating women, while separately coded conversations can recover barriers documented by traditional instruments and reveal additional reproducible information about the care-seeking journey. For women who report an outcome, timestamps may also support exploratory measurement of the interval from first expressed need to self-reported care. Specifically:

**H1 (measurement).** Follow-up items specified in advance will produce self-reported antenatal and vaccination attendance measures meeting protocol-prespecified thresholds for completeness and reliability, with acceptable response process and content validity.

**H2 (added information).** Conversational data will recover the barrier categories documented by survey-based studies of refugee women's access to care[3] and identify barriers those instruments do not capture, with inter-coder reproducibility meeting prespecified thresholds.

**H3 (exploratory, directional).** Among participating women, the interval from first expressed need to self-reported care will be longer for women who report access barriers than for women who do not.

*Nonresponse is treated as missing data and analyzed for its effect on these estimates; it is never interpreted as missed care.*

**Aim 1. Test whether the service's follow-up questions work as measures.** An audit of the existing de-identified data (1a) first establishes what each question asks, its denominator, how many women answer it, whether timestamps are available, and whether the data can be exported; it is the go/no-go gate for everything after it and supplies all corpus figures. A prospective component (1b), under its own IRB protocol and consent route, then redesigns the questions as a deliberate instrument (wording, timing, placement) and evaluates response process, content validity, completeness, and reliability. The measured construct is **self-reported utilization**; no external reference standard is assumed. *Outputs: audit report; prespecified instrument; measurement-properties evaluation; methods manuscript.*

**Aim 2. Test what conversations add beyond traditional instruments, and map the journey.** A barrier taxonomy is coded from what women write, anchored to the barrier categories that survey-based studies have documented:[3] the test is whether conversational data recover what traditional instruments capture and reveal what they miss (H2). Lay-language health terminology, including dialectal Arabic, is evaluated where the corpus supports it. Where timestamps are exportable, the time from first expressed need to self-reported care is measured and compared across barrier groups (H3). All utilization analyses use a three-state outcome (self-reported attendance, self-reported nonattendance, no response), and the analysis reports whom the data represent and whom they miss. *Outputs: barrier taxonomy; need-to-care interval analysis; three-state utilization analyses; terminology brief.*

**Expected outcomes and success criteria.**

## 1. Transformative Potential and Impact

**Significance.** Health systems, and the information systems built on them, are designed around people who are born, treated, and counted in the same place. They are poorly designed for refugee women whose care moves across cities, providers, and documentation systems. Facility data can describe the women who arrive; they say much less about those who seek care but do not reach it: a blind spot created by systems that count service contacts rather than population care-seeking pathways.[15]

Approximately 2.25 million Syrian refugees remain under temporary protection in Türkiye.[11] Syrian women retain formal access to Ministry of Health migrant health centers, conditional on registration status and geography; since January 2026, contribution rules have shifted while formal access remains, adding entitlement and co-payment complexity to language, health-system literacy, and navigation barriers.[11-12] Documented barriers to preventive care persist across the evidence base.[3] It has been estimated that up-to-date immunization levels are under 65% and prenatal check-up appointments remain below 30% among the Syrian refugee population in Turkey.[1] Clinical findings are heterogeneous, with persistent maternal and newborn inequalities in a 2025 meta-analysis,[4] making modifiable access pathways the plausible study target.

HERA, a mobile health intervention previously developed and piloted with Syrian refugees in Türkiye,[1-2] now operates as an AI-supported WhatsApp navigation service for Arabic-speaking refugee women, available around the clock on their own phones without travel or appointments. Prior evidence shows that women have access to smartphones and use them to seek health information.[1-2] The service is designed as a culturally adapted, non-clinical companion in dialectal Arabic. When a woman registers a pregnancy or child, HERA generates the corresponding antenatal or host-country vaccination schedule, sends reminders, answers questions using verified maternal and child health content, locates nearby facilities, and stores personal health records. Routine use also includes three structured follow-up cycles: caregiver-reported vaccination attendance, self-reported antenatal check-up attendance, and periodic questions about access to health services.

In routine systems, observation usually begins when a woman reaches a provider;[15] in HERA, observation can begin at the moment a woman first expresses a need and continue through a sequence of questions, information, referrals, reminders, and follow-up. The closest precedents are consumer-scale intent classification without linkage to subsequent care-seeking[6] and a chatbot-evaluation literature focused on advice quality rather than measurement validity;[9] we have identified no study using conversational service records as utilization-measurement instruments in a refugee population. A documented scoping search in months 1–2 will test and bound this novelty claim.

Prior work on HERA defines both the promise and the risk. A quasi-experimental evaluation under peer review (app era, two Istanbul migrant health centers) found higher mean antenatal attendance among women with confirmed reminder exposure, 2.5 versus 1.9 visits, but the planned randomized design failed because identifiers were incompletely captured in routine operations.[5] That failure is this proposal's founding lesson: operational data become research data only through prospective definitions, governance, and linkage discipline.

**Innovation and impact.** The contribution is a measurement discipline for humanitarian conversational data: prespecifying which record types serve as outcomes, which as qualitative evidence, which as exposures, and which as missingness, and evaluating each accordingly. Current health-chatbot literature largely lacks this discipline, and language and cultural bias are documented risks in these populations.[7-9] Studying whom digital service data omit, through response-propensity and selection analysis, is itself a needed methods contribution: the women a phone-based service cannot see are often exactly those whom community health workers and door-to-door outreach exist to reach.

## 2. Strategic Alignment

The project integrates AI and data science (natural language processing, annotation design, and sensitivity modeling) with population-health measurement in a displaced population, within life-sciences scope through maternal and child health. The award funds a DrPH dissertation and a controlled, annotated JHU research dataset with documented governance; no open-access corpus is proposed.

## 3. Strength of Approach and Feasibility

**Aim 1.** The audit (1a, months 1–6) runs under an IRB secondary-analysis determination and answers one question: are the existing data good enough to study? Cycles that cannot be verified are dropped (**data gate**). Component 1b (months 5–12, separate protocol and consent) prespecifies item wording and timing, checks comprehension through cognitive interviews (n≈20–30), two to three focus groups, and up to fifteen in-depth interviews with consented service users, and evaluates response process, content validity, completeness, and reliability. Items meeting prespecified response and completeness thresholds enter Aim 2; failures are reported as findings.

**Aim 2.** The coding framework starts from the barrier categories documented in the survey-based literature,[3] so recovery of known barriers and discovery of new ones are both prespecified endpoints. Trained annotators, with clinician adjudication, label a stratified subset of conversations (several hundred; final n set in the protocol) to build the taxonomy; agreement between coders is measured (target kappa ≥ 0.70), and automated classification is evaluated per language against a held-out test set (precision, recall, F1). The interval analysis (H3) proceeds only if the audit confirms timestamps are exportable. No representativeness correction is claimed without an identified benchmark.

**Ethics and data architecture.** Users provide explicit consent at signup; the monitoring platform is GDPR-compliant by design and captures only user messages and bot responses. HERA's operational evaluation runs under existing ethics approval (ATADEK 2024-17/650, Acıbadem University, 2024), and prior studies in this population were ethics-approved and trial-registered.[1,5] The protocol adds a research layer under JHU IRB: component-level determinations, a Türkiye-consistent data-use agreement, platform-side de-identification with linkage keys never leaving the platform, and analysis in a controlled JHU environment. Clinician annotation carries a higher privacy burden than eyes-off consumer precedents,[6] addressed through minimization, pseudonymization, and access controls. Caregiver-reported child vaccination data are treated as children's health information. Research review of flagged conversations is retrospective, with survivor-centered escalation for gender-based violence and acute-risk disclosures; real-time safety response remains a service function. Declining research participation will never affect service access. The trainee's dual role (platform founder, DrPH student) is disclosed: data extraction by platform staff under agreement; analyses independently supervised under the PI.

**Timeline and gates.** Months 1–2: IRB submissions, scoping search, agreements (**rights gate**). Months 3–6: audit (**data gate**). Months 5–12: prospective instrument study (**measurement gate**). Months 10–20: Aim 2 analyses (**selection gate**). Months 18–24: manuscripts and dissemination.

**Feasibility.** JHU hosts oversight, analysis, trainee support, and a controlled data environment. JHU-led validation activities in Türkiye (cognitive interviews, focus groups, and in-depth interviews) run under separate IRB with CHH supervision; the platform partner provides opt-in recruitment pathways and de-identified data within the 10% external cap, not field supervision or primary data collection.

## 4. Team Capability and Environment

**Shatha Elnakib (Lead PI, CHH):** mixed-methods research on refugee integration into national health systems and humanitarian reproductive health service delivery;[13-14] owns study design, ethics, the validation framework, and publication. **CHH research analyst (award-funded):** executes NLP, statistics, and data governance under the PI. **Aral Surmeli (DrPH trainee; advisor William Weiss):** first author of the Istanbul evaluation and registered PI of NCT05094518;[5] leads data-provenance mapping and audit documentation; trainee beneficiary, not co-PI; founder conflict managed as described. **HERA Digital Health (external platform partner):** de-identified data provision and bounded integration at or below 10% of the award, or in-kind; no field research role. A data-science or biostatistics faculty co-investigator may be added at the PI's discretion.

## 5. Future Funding Potential

The long-term question is comparative: can an AI-supported navigation service connect refugee women to care more effectively than episodic, encounter-based approaches? Conditional on its gates, this award produces the assets that study requires and does not yet have: a measurement-evaluated instrument, a controlled annotated dataset with known properties, quantified nonresponse and selection behavior, a defensible barrier evidence profile, and a quantified distribution of time from expressed need to self-reported care among participating refugee women. Follow-on directions include DIV, FID, NIH, foundation implementation-research mechanisms, and other staged evaluation funders.

## 6. Appropriate Use of Institutional Funds

Funds prioritize JHU scientific and analyst effort, DrPH support, Türkiye field validation travel, participant incentives, secure compute, annotation, and dissemination; the platform partner remains at or below the 10% external cap ($35,000 at this request level).

## 7. Demonstrated Need for Support

The measurement foundation this study builds does not yet exist and is prerequisite to any adequately powered external evaluation; producing it is precisely the catalytic role of institutional support.
